\section{The Postulates of Special Relativity}
\label{section:postulates}

\begin{enumerate} 

\item The laws of physics are the same for all observers in
uniform motion relative to each other (i.e., in inertial
reference frames).  This means that no inertial observer can
detect a preferred or absolute state of motion. There is no
absolute "rest" frame; all motion is relative.

\item The speed of light in a vacuum is always constant,
regardless of the motion of the observer or the light
source. This speed is approximately $c=299,792,458$ m/s.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Time Dilation}
\label{section:timedialation}
Time dilation is an unusual phenomenon predicted by Einstein's
special theory of relativity.  It refers to the effect where time
appears to pass more slowly for an observer in motion relative to
a stationary observer. In simpler terms, a moving clock ticks
more slowly compared to a clock that is at rest, as observed by
someone who is not moving with the clock.

\bigskip
\noindent
The key idea is that when an object moves at a speed close to the
speed of light relative to an observer, time for the moving
object (as seen by the observer) slows down. This effect becomes
more pronounced as the speed of the object approaches the speed
of light.

\noindent
For observer at rest with respect to an inertial frame of
reference, the time taken for a beam of light to travel from the
light source to the detector is

\vspace{-3.0mm}
\begin{equation*}
  \begin{array}{llll}
     h
     &=& c \Delta t_0 					
     		&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#} \Delta t_0 \text{ is the time for light to travel from the source to the detector} \\
[10pt]
	 &\Rightarrow& \Delta t_0 = \dfrac{h}{c} 
	 		&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#} \text{solve for $\Delta t_0$}
  \end{array}
\end{equation*}

\smallskip
\noindent
The question now is how much time, call it $\Delta t$, passes
from the point of view of observer $O$ when the light travels
from the source to the detector. We know that the velocity of
light (at least in a vaccum) is $c$, so distance that the light
travels in this case is $c \Delta t$. So now we can use the
Pythagorean theorem to solve for $\Delta t$ as follows:


\begin{equation*}
 \begin{array}{llll}
  (c \Delta t)^2
     &=& h^2 + (\Delta x)^2 					
     		&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#} \text{by the Pythagorean theorem} \\
[15pt]
     &\Rightarrow& (c \Delta t)^2 = {(c\Delta t_0)}^2 + (\Delta x)^2 					
     		&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#} h = c \Delta t_0 \\
[15pt]
	&\Rightarrow& (c \Delta t)^2 = (c \Delta t_{0})^2 + (v \Delta t)^2
			&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#} \text{velocity $v$ of the train: } v = \dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \Rightarrow \Delta x = v \Delta t \\
[15pt]
	&\Rightarrow& c^2 (\Delta t)^2 = c^2 (\Delta t_{0})^2 + v^2 (\Delta t)^2
		&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#}  \text{square terms} \\
[15pt]
	&\Rightarrow& (\Delta t)^2 = (\Delta t_{0})^2 + \dfrac{v^2}{c^2} (\Delta t)^2
			&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#}  \text{divide both sides by $c^2$} \\
[15pt]
	&\Rightarrow& (\Delta t)^2 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2} (\Delta t)^2 = (\Delta t_{0})^2 
			&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#}  \text{subtract $\dfrac{v^2}{c^2} (\Delta t)^2$ from both sides} \\
[15pt]
	&\Rightarrow& (\Delta t)^2 \left (1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2} \right) = (\Delta t_{0})^2 
			&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#} \text{factor out $(\Delta t)^2$} \\
[15pt]	
	&\Rightarrow& (\Delta t)^2 = \dfrac{(\Delta t_{0})^2}{\left (1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2} \right)}
		&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#}  \text{divide both sides by $\left (1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2}\right )$} \\
[35pt]
	&\Rightarrow& \Delta t = \dfrac{\Delta t_{0}}{\sqrt{1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2}}}
		&\hspace{2em} \mathrel{\#}  \text{take the square root of both sides}
  \end{array}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
So for $O$ the passage of time on the moving train slows down by a factor of

\medskip
\begin{equation}
  \gamma = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-\dfrac{v^2}{c^2}}}
  \label{equation:lorentz_factor}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Equation ({\ref{equation:lorentz_factor}) is called the Lorentz
factor (or sometimes the gamma factor).
