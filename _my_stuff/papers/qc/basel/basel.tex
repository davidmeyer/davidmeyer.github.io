\section{Introduction}
The Basel problem \cite{wiki:basel} is a problem in mathematical analysis with relevance to number theory that first posed by Pietro Mengoli in 1650 and solved by 
Leonhard Euler in 1734. 

\bigskip
\noindent
The Basel problem asks whether the infinite sum

\begin{equation*}
\mathlarger{\sum\limits_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{1}{1^2}  + \frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} + \cdots}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
evaluates to a finite number, and if it does, what does it converge to? An example of a related divergent series is the harmonic series \cite{wiki:harmonic}:

\begin{equation*}
\mathlarger{\sum\limits_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} = \frac{1}{1} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \cdots = \infty}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Both sum in the Basel problem and the harmonic series are instances of the famous Riemann zeta function \cite{wiki:zeta}, $\zeta(s)$. The harmonic series is $\zeta(1)$
and the Basel problem is $\zeta(2)$. For example

\begin{equation*}
\mathlarger{ \zeta(2) = \sum\limits_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{1}{1^2}  + \frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} + \cdots}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
The Basel problem resisted solution for some 84 years before the then 26 year old Euler finally solved it. Euler's surprising solution
was

\bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\mathlarger{\sum\limits_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{1}{1^2}  + \frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} + \cdots = \frac{\pi^2}{6}}
\end{equation*}

\section{Euler's Solution}
So how did Euler solve the Basel problem? The summary is that his extraordinary mathematical intuition led him to look at the Maclauren series \cite{wiki:taylor}
for $\sin(x)$ and comparing its coefficients to a polynomial constructed from the zeros a polynomial for $\sin(x)$ (as indicated by the Fundamental 
Theory of Algebra \cite{wiki:ftoa} or more specifically, Weierstrass factorization \cite{wiki:weierstrass}. So how exactly did Euler arrive at his fantastic solution?

\subsection{Maclaurin (Taylor) Series for $\sin(x)$}
The story begins with the  Maclaurin series for $\sin(x)$. This series was well-known in Euler's time. 
Note that we are using the $f^{(n)}$ notation for the $n^{\text{th}}$ derivative of $f$, where $f^{(0)} = f$. 

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{lllll}
\sin(x)
&=& \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!} x^n                                                                                                                  &\mathrel{\#} \text{Maclaurin series for $\sin(x)$}              \\
[12pt]
&=& \frac{f^{(0)}(0)}{0!} x^0 +  \frac{f^{(1)}(0)}{1!} x^1 +  \frac{f^{(2)}(0)}{2!} x^2 + \frac{f^{(3)}(0)}{3!} x^3 + \cdots                     &\mathrel{\#} \text{expansion of terms}                               \\
[12pt] 
&=& \frac{\sin(0)}{0!}x^0 + \frac{\cos(0)}{1!}x^1 +  \frac{-\sin(0)}{2!}x^2 +  \frac{-\cos(0)}{3!}x^3 +  \frac{\sin(0)}{4!} x^4 \cdots  &\mathrel{\#} f(x) = \sin(x)                                                    \\
[12pt]
&=& 0 +  (\frac{1}{1!}) x^1 + 0 + (\frac{-1}{3!}) x^3+ 0 + (\frac{1}{5!}) x^5 + \cdots                                                                       &\mathrel{\#} \text{the sequence is $\{0,1,0,-1,...\}$}           \\
[12pt]
&=& \sum\limits_{k = 0}^{\infty} (-1)^k  \frac{x^{2k+1}}{(k+1)!}                                                                                                     &\mathrel{\#} \text{note that here $k=0$ is the $x^1$ term}
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Euler's key insight was to observe that if we can find a polynomial for $\sin(x)$ then the coefficients of terms of the same power in the MacLaurin series and the polynomial 
must equal one another.

\subsection{Euler's Polynomial for $\sin(x)$}
We know from the Fundamental Theory of Algebra \cite{wiki:ftoa} and the Weierstrass Factorization Theorem \cite{wiki:weierstrass} that every polynomial has at least complex root.
As a result if there is a polynomial for $\sin(x)$ and knowing that the zeros of $\sin(x)$ occur at $0, \pm \pi, \pm 2\pi, \hdots$ we can directly write down a polynomial for $\sin(x)$:

\begin{equation*}
\sin(x) = A x (x - \pi)(x + \pi) (x - 2\pi)(x + 2\pi) (x - 3\pi) (x + 3\pi) \cdots
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Here the tricky part is finding $A$. So how did Euler do it? Euler noticed that for infinitesimally small values of $x$ $\sin(x) = x$, or said in a different way\footnote{Euler is said 
to have said that "$\sin(x) = x$ when $x$ is infinitesimally small".} 

\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = 1
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
We also know that

\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{x (x - \pi)(x+\pi)(x - 2\pi)(x + 2\pi)(x - 3\pi)(x + 3\pi) \cdots}{x} = (-\pi^2)(-(2\pi)^2)(-(3\pi)^2) \cdots
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Together these imply that

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{lllll}
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = 1
&\Rightarrow& 1 = A (x - \pi)(x + \pi) (x - 2\pi)(x+2\pi) \cdots                                     &\mathrel{\#} \text{polynomial for $\frac{\sin(x)}{x}$ as $x \to 0$}   \\
[10pt]
&\Rightarrow& A = \dfrac{1}{(x - \pi)(x + \pi) (x - 2\pi)(x+2\pi) \cdots }                       &\mathrel{\#} \text{solve for $A$}                                                    \\
[10pt]
&\Rightarrow& A = \dfrac{1}{(-(1\pi)^2)(-(2\pi)^2) (-1 (3\pi)^2) \cdots }                       &\mathrel{\#} \text{set $x = 0$}                                                     \\
[10pt]
&\Rightarrow& A = \dfrac{1}{(-\pi^2) (- 2^2 \pi^2) (-3^2 \pi^2)  \cdots}                        &\mathrel{\#} \text{simplify}  
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
So now we know that 

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{lllll}
\sin (x) 
&=&  A x (x - \pi)(x + \pi) (x - 2\pi)(x + 2\pi) (x - 3\pi) (x + 3\pi) \cdots                                                                                                                          &\mathrel{\#} \text{definition of the polynomial}                              \\   
[14pt]                                                     
&=& \dfrac{x (x - \pi)(x+\pi)(x-2\pi)(x+2\pi)(x-3\pi)(x+3\pi) \cdots}{(-\pi^2)(-2^2\pi^2)(-3^2\pi^2) \cdots}                                                                      &\mathrel{\#} \text{polynomial for $\frac{\sin(x)}{x}$ as $x \to 0$}  \\
[14pt]
&=& \dfrac{x (x^2 - \pi^2)(x^2-4\pi^2)(x^2 - 9\pi^2) \cdots}{(-\pi^2)(-2^2\pi^2)(-3^2\pi^2) \cdots}                                                                                &\mathrel{\#} \text{multiply through}                                                \\
[14pt]
&=& x \cdot \Bigg [\dfrac{(\pi^2 - x^2)}{\pi^2} \Bigg ] \cdot  \Bigg [\dfrac{(4 \pi^2 -  x^2)}{4 \pi^2} \Bigg ]  \cdot  \Bigg [\dfrac{(9 \pi^2 - x^2)}{9 \pi^2} \Bigg ]  \cdots &\mathrel{\#} \text{collect terms}                             \\
[14pt]
&=& x \cdot  \Bigg [1 - \dfrac{x^2}{\pi^2} \Bigg ] \cdot \Bigg [1 - \dfrac{x^2}{4\pi^2} \Bigg ] \cdot  \Bigg [1 - \dfrac{x^2}{9\pi^2} \Bigg ] \cdots           &\mathrel{\#} \text{simplify}                                                             \\
[14pt]
&\Rightarrow& \dfrac{\sin(x)}{x} = \Bigg [1 - \dfrac{x^2}{\pi^2} \Bigg ] \cdot \Bigg [1 - \dfrac{x^2}{4\pi^2} \Bigg ] \cdot  \Bigg [1 - \dfrac{x^2}{9\pi^2} \Bigg ] \cdots &\mathrel{\#} \text{divide both sides by $x$}
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Said another way

\begin{equation}
\sin(x) = x \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \Bigg (1 - \frac{x^2}{(n\pi)^2} \Bigg )
\label{eqn:sine}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Euler also noticed that since the MacLaurin series for $\sin(x)$ is 

\begin{equation*}
\sin(x) = \sum\limits_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^2 \frac{x^{(2n+1)}} {(2n+1)!}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
if we multiply by $\sin(x)$ by $\frac{1}{x}$ term by term we get 

\bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\sin(x)}{x} = \sum\limits_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n \Bigg [\frac{1}{x} \Bigg ]\frac{x^{(2n+1)}} {(2n+1)!} = \sum\limits_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{x^{2n}} {(2n+1)!} 
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Looking at the coefficients of the $x^2$ terms of $\frac{\sin(x)}{x}$, we see that in the MacLauren series we have $\frac{1}{3!} = \frac{1}{6}$ and in the polynomial we have

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{1}{\pi^2} \sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{1}{n^2}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Since the coefficients of the $x^2$ terms in the MacLaurin series and the polynomial must be equal, we see that

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{1}{6} = \dfrac{1}{\pi^2} \sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{1}{n^2}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
and multiplying both sides by $\pi^2$ we get

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{\pi^2}{6} = \sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{1}{n^2}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
which amazingly is Euler's solution to the Basel problem.

\section{Interesting Consequences}
The first thing we can notice is what happens when we plug $\frac{\pi}{2}$ into Equation \ref{eqn:sine}. Noticing that $\sin \big (\frac{\pi}{2} \big ) = 1$ and with
a bit of rearranging  we see that


\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{lllll}
 \sin(x)
&=&  \mathlarger{x \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \Bigg (1 - \dfrac{x^2}{(n\pi)^2} \Bigg )}                                              &\qquad \mathrel{\#}  \text{Equation \ref{eqn:sine}} \\
[15pt]
&=& \mathlarger{x \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \Bigg (\dfrac{n^2\pi^2 - x^2}{n^2\pi^2} \Bigg )}                                   &\qquad \mathrel{\#}  \text{get common denominator, multiply out } \\
[15pt]
&\Rightarrow& \mathlarger{\sin \bigg (\dfrac{\pi}{2} \bigg ) = \dfrac{\pi}{2} \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \Bigg (\dfrac{n^2\pi^2 - \frac{\pi^2}{4}}{n^2\pi^2} \Bigg )} 
                                                                                                                                                                               &\qquad \mathrel{\#}  \text{set $\mathlarger{x = \frac{\pi}{2}}$} \\
[15pt]
&\Rightarrow& \mathlarger{1 =  \dfrac{\pi}{2}  \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \Bigg (\pi^2 \dfrac{ \big ( n^2 - \frac{1}{4}  \big )} {n^2 \pi^2} \Bigg )} 
                                                                                                                                                                               &\qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{$\sin \bigg (\dfrac{\pi}{2} \bigg ) =1 $, factor out $\pi^2$} \\
[15pt]
&\Rightarrow& \mathlarger{1 =  \dfrac{\pi}{2}  \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \Bigg (\pi^2 \dfrac{ \big ( \frac{4n^2 - 1}{4}  \big )} {n^2 \pi^2} \Bigg )} &\qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{get common denomintor} \\
[15pt]
&\Rightarrow&  \mathlarger{1 = \dfrac{\pi}{2}  \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \Bigg ( \dfrac{ \big ( \frac{4n^2 - 1}{4}  \big )} {n^2} \Bigg )}      &\qquad \mathrel{\#}  \text{cancel $\pi^2$} \\
[15pt]
&\Rightarrow& \mathlarger{1 = \dfrac{\pi}{2} \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \bigg ( \frac {4n^2 - 1} {4n^2} \bigg )}         &\qquad \mathrel{\#}  \text{multiply right side by $\mathlarger{\frac{\frac{1}{n^2}}{\frac{1}{n^2}}}$} \\
[15pt]
 &\Rightarrow&  \mathlarger{\dfrac{2}{\pi} = \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \bigg (\frac {4n^2 - 1} {4n^2} \bigg )}            &\qquad \mathrel{\#}  \text{multiply both sides by $\mathlarger{\frac{2}{\pi}}$} \\
 [15pt]
 &\Rightarrow& \mathlarger{\frac{\pi}{2}  =  \prod\limits_{n=1}^\infty \bigg ( \frac  {4n^2} {4n^2 - 1} \bigg )}           &\qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{$x = y \Rightarrow \dfrac{1}{x} = \dfrac{1}{y}$ where $x,y \neq 0$} \\
[15pt]
&\Rightarrow& \mathlarger{\frac{\pi}{2}  = \prod\limits_{n =1}^\infty \Bigg  (\frac{(2n)(2n)}{(2n-1)(2n+1)}  \Bigg )}  &\qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{factor numerator and denominator}
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
So we wind up with

\begin{equation*}
\frac{\pi}{2}  
= \prod\limits_{n =1}^\infty \Bigg  ( \frac{(2n)(2n)}{(2n-1)(2n+1)}  \Bigg ) 
= \Big ( \frac {2}{1} \cdot \frac {2}{3} \Big ) \cdot \Big ( \frac {4}{3} \cdot \frac {4}{5} \Big ) \cdot \Big (\frac {6}{5} \cdot \frac {6}{7} \Big ) \cdot \Big (\frac {8}{7} \cdot \frac {8}{9} \Big ) \cdots 
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
that is, the Wallis Product \cite{wiki:wallis}.
