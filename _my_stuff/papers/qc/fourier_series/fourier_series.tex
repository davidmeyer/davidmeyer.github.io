 \bigskip
 \noindent
 Some people think that the orthonormal basis for the Fourier
 series should be $\{{\bf 1}, \sqrt{2} \cos mt,\sqrt{2} \sin
 nt\}$, since
 
 
\begin{equation*}
  \langle {\bf 1},{\bf 1} \rangle = 
     \frac{1}{\pi}\int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} {\bf 1}^2 \, dt = 
     \frac{1}{\pi}\int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} dt              = 
     \frac{1}{\pi}  \, t \, \big |^{\pi}_{-\pi}            = 
     \frac{1}{\pi} \left (\pi - (-\pi) \right )            = 
     \frac{1}{\pi} 2\pi                                    = 
     2
\end{equation*}
 
 \noindent
 On the other hand, $\langle \cos nt, \cos nt \rangle = {\displaystyle
 \dfrac{1}{\pi} \int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos^2 nt \,dt =
 \dfrac{1}{\pi} \pi = 1}$. In the same way, $\langle \sin nt,
 \sin nt \rangle = 1$.
 
 \medskip
 \noindent
 One of the points here (or perhaps, the point here) is that
 $\langle \hat{\mathbf{u}},\hat{\mathbf{u}} \rangle = 1$ for all
 unit vectors $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$, so what about $\langle {\bf
 1},{\bf 1} \rangle$ if {\bf 1} is part of an orthonormal basis
 (again, since $\langle {\bf 1},{\bf 1} \rangle = 2$)?
 
 
\bigskip
\noindent
 Note also that here
 
\begin{equation*}
  \langle {\bf 1},\sin nt \rangle = {\displaystyle
  \dfrac{1}{\pi}\int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} {\bf 1} \sin nt \, dt} =
  {\displaystyle \dfrac{1}{\pi}\int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin nt
  \, dt}  = 0 
\end{equation*} 
  
\bigskip
\noindent
Similarly, $\langle {\bf 1},\cos nt \rangle = \langle \cos
mt,\sin nt \rangle = 0$.
