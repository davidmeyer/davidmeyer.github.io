\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
The classic English Christmas carol "The Twelve Days of
Christmas" has all kinds of interesting mathematical
features. First, it is an example of a \emph{cumulative song}, in
which the lyrics detail a series of increasingly numerous gifts
given to the speaker by their "true love" on each of the twelve
days of Christmas (the twelve days that make up the Christmas
season, starting with Christmas Day)
\cite{wikipedia:cumulative_song}.  So we might want to know how
many gifts are given each day and what the total number of gifts
are.

\bigskip
\noindent
The carol, whose words were apparently first published in England
in the late eighteenth century, has a Roud Folk Song Index number
of 68 \cite{wikipedia:roud_folk_song_index}. A large number of
different melodies have been associated with the song, of which
the best known is derived from a 1909 arrangement of a
traditional folk melody by English composer Frederic Austin
\cite{wikipedia:twelve_days_of_christmas}. There are twelve
verses, each describing a gift given by "my true love" on one of
the twelve days of Christmas. There are many variations in the
lyrics, however, the lyrics given here are from Frederic Austin's
1909 publication that established the current form of the carol
\cite{wikipedia:frederic_austin}.

\bigskip
\noindent
The first three verses run, in full, as follows:

\bigskip
\begin{itemize}
\item [] On the first day of Christmas my true love sent to me:
\emph{A partridge in a pear tree}.

\item [] On the second day of Christmas my true love sent to me:
\emph{Two turtle doves, and a partridge in a pear tree}.

\item [] On the third day of Christmas my true love sent to me:
\emph{Three French hens, two turtle doves, and a partridge in 
a pear tree}.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip
\noindent
The subsequent verses follow the same pattern. Each verse deals
with the next day of Christmastide, adding one new gift and then
repeating all the earlier gifts, so that each verse is one line
longer than its predecessor.

\begin{itemize}
\item 4 calling birds
\item 5 gold rings
\item 6 geese a-laying
\item 7 swans a-swimming
\item 8 maids a-milking
\item 9 ladies dancing
\item 10 lords a-leaping
\item 11 pipers piping
\item 12 drummers drumming
\end{itemize}

\section{How many gifts are received each day and what is the total?}
\bigskip
\noindent
The number of gifts received on day {\bf n} and the total number
of gifts received by day {\bf n} are shown in the following table
$(1 \leq {\mathbf n} \leq 12)$:

    \label{tab:12_days_of_christmas}
  \end{table}

\noindent
Interestingly, the numbers in blue in Table
\ref{tab:12_days_of_christmas}, the number of gifts received on
day {\bf n}, are known as the triangular numbers. The
$n^{\text{th}}$ triangular number, $T_{n}$, is defined as
follows:


\bigskip
\begin{equation}
T_{n}=\sum _{k=1}^{n}k = \frac {n(n+1)}{2} = {n+1 \choose 2}
\label{eqn:triangular_number}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
where ${\displaystyle \binom{n}{m}}$ is the familiar binomial
coefficient\footnote{The great mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss
is said to have discovered the consecutive integer formula
(Equation (\ref{eqn:triangular_number})) while still at primary
school \cite{add_1_to_100}.}. For example, $T_{12} = 
\sum\limits_{k=1}^{12} k = \frac{12 \cdot 13}{2} = {13 \choose 2} = 
\mathbf{\color{blue} 78}$.

\bigskip
\noindent
So how many total gifts have been received by day {\bf n}? These
numbers are shown in red in Table
\ref{tab:12_days_of_christmas}. These numbers are known as
tetrahedral numbers. The $n^{\text{th}}$ tetrahedral number,
$T_{e_n}$, is defined as follows:

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
T_{e_n} = \sum\limits_{k=1}^{n} T_k = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{6} = 
          \binom{n+2}{3}
\label{eqn:tetrahedral_number}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
where $T_{k}$ is the $k^{\text{th}}$ triangular number 
(see Equation (\ref{eqn:triangular_number})).

\bigskip
\noindent
It turns out that the total number of gifts received is
$T_{e_{12}} = \sum\limits_{k=1}^{12} T_k = \frac{12 \cdot 13 
\cdot 14}{6} = \binom{14}{3} = \mathbf{\color{red}{364}}$.

\noindent
Also interesting is the connection to Pascal's triangle
\cite{wiki:pascals_triangle}.  Specifically, the $4^{\text{th}}$
diagonal row of Pascal's triangle (starting from the right side)
contains the tetrahedral numbers, $T_{e_n}$, that is, the
$4^{\text{th}}$ diagonal row of Pascal's triangle contains the
{\bf Running total at end of day n}. This is shown in Figure
\ref{fig:lua_pascals_triangle}.  Similarly, the $3^{\text{rd}}$
diagonal row of Pascal's triangle contains the numbers we see in
{\bf Gifts received on day n} column of Table
\ref{tab:12_days_of_christmas}. These are the triangular numbers
$T_n$.

\section{Geometric Interpretations}
There are an interesting geometric interpretation of both
triangular numbers and tetrahedral numbers. So first, what
exactly is a triangular number?  The first six triangular numbers
are depicted as triangles in Figure \ref{fig:triangular_numbers}.
We can see from the figure that a triangular number $T_n$ is the
sum of the integers from 1 to $n$ \cite{wiki:triangle_numbers}.
This is also the number of gifts received on the $n^{\text{th}}$
day of Christmas according to the "12 Days of Christmas".
  
\vspace{1.0em}
\begin{figure}[H]
  \center{\includegraphics[frame,scale=0.40] {images/first_six_triangular_numbers.png}}
  \caption{The First Six Triangular Numbers}
  \label{fig:triangular_numbers}
\end{figure}

\noindent
Next, what are the tetrahedral numbers? As we saw in Equation
(\ref{eqn:tetrahedral_number}), the $n^{\text{th}}$ tetrahedral
number is the sum of the first $n$ triangular numbers
\cite{wolfram:tetrahedral_numbers}. The $n^{\text{th}}$
tetrahedral is the number of gifts received up to and including
day $n^{\text{th}}$ of the 12 Days of Christmas. These numbers
are depicted in Figure \ref{fig:tetrahedral_numbers}.

\vspace{2.5em}
\begin{figure}[H]
  \center{\includegraphics[frame,scale=0.30] {images/tetrahedral_numbers.png}}
  \caption{The Tetrahedral Numbers}
  \label{fig:tetrahedral_numbers}
\end{figure}
