\section{Introduction}
In the 1730s Leonhard Euler proved an important theorem, known today as Euler's Product Formula  \cite{euler_product_formula}, which exposes a deep 
relationship between the prime numbers and the Riemann zeta function \cite{wiki:zeta}. Euler's proof appeared in his thesis titled  
\emph{Variae observationes circa series infinitas} (Various Observations about Infinite Series), which was published by the 
St. Petersburg Academy in 1737 \cite{mactutor:history_of_calculus}. Euler's proof of the product formula was an important advancement and
is perhaps the main reason why the zeta function plays such a central role in the study of prime numbers.

\bigskip
\noindent
The Euler product formula for the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)$ is

\begin{equation*}
\zeta (s)=\sum _{n=1}^{\infty }{\frac {1}{n^{s}}}=\prod _{p{\text{ prime}}}{\frac {1}{1-p^{-s}}}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Here the left hand side is the famous Riemann zeta function:

\begin{equation*}
\zeta (s) = \sum _{n=1}^{\infty } \frac {1}{n^{s}} = 1 + \frac {1}{2^{s}} + \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \cdots 
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
and the product on the right hand side extends over all prime numbers $p$:

\begin{equation*}
\prod _{p {\text{ prime}}}  \frac {1}{1-p^{-s}} = \frac {1}{1-2^{-s}} \cdot \frac {1}{1-3^{-s}} \cdot \frac {1}{1-5^{-s}}  \cdots  \frac {1}{1-p^{-s}} \cdots 
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
So how did Euler find the amazing relationship between the zeta function and the primes?

\section{Sketch of a Simple Proof of the Euler Product Formula}
This proof sketched here makes use only of simple algebra and is the method by which Euler originally discovered the product formula. The proof takes advantage of a 
sieving property that can be traced to the ancient Sieve of Eratosthenes \cite{wiki:sieve_of_eratosthenes} and proceeds as follows. 

\bigskip
\noindent
First, we write down the zeta function:

\begin{equation}
\zeta(s) = \sum _{n=1}^{\infty } \frac {1}{n^{s}} = 1 + \frac {1}{2^{s}} + \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \frac{1}{4^s} + \frac{1}{5^2} + \cdots    
\label{equation1}  
\end{equation}


\bigskip
\noindent
Next, we make a copy of Equation \ref{equation1} and multiply both sides by the first term of Equation \ref{equation1}\footnote{We ignore the leading 1 in this process.}, 
$\frac{1}{2^s}$ 

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\frac {1}{2^{s}} \zeta (s) = \frac {1}{2^{s}} + \frac {1}{4^{s}} + \frac {1}{6^{s}} + \frac {1}{8^{s}} + \frac {1}{10^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation2}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Now we subtract Equation \ref{equation2} from Equation \ref{equation1}, which knocks out all terms that have a factor of 2. The result looks like

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\left (1- \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = 1 + \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \frac {1}{5^{s}} + \frac {1}{7^{s}} + \frac {1}{9^{s}} + \frac {1}{11^{s}} + \frac {1}{13^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation3}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Next we multiply Equation \ref{equation3} by the next term on the right hand side of Equation \ref{equation3}, $\frac{1}{3^s}$, which gives us

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\frac {1}{3^{s}} \left (1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \frac {1}{9^{s}} + \frac {1}{15^{s}} + \frac {1}{21^{s}} + \frac {1}{27^{s}} + \frac {1}{33^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation4}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Subtracting Equation \ref{equation4} from Equation \ref{equation3} we get

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\left ( 1 - \frac {1}{3^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = 1 + \frac {1}{5^{s}} + \frac {1}{7^{s}} + \frac {1}{11^{s}} + \frac {1}{13^{s}} + \frac {1}{17^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation5}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
which knocks out all of the terms on the right with a factor of 3. What we can see is that the right side is being sieved in a process that is analogous to the Sieve of Eratosthenes.

\bigskip
\noindent
If we repeat this process infinitely for $\frac{1}{p^2}$ where $p$ is prime we wind up with

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\cdots \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{11^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{7^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{5^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{3^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = 1
\label{sieved_euler_product_formula}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
If we now solve for $\zeta(s)$ we get

\bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\zeta (s) = \frac {1}{\left ( 1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{3^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{5^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{7^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{11^{s}} \right ) \cdots}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\bigskip
\noindent
This can be written in a perhaps more familiar way as an infinite product over all the primes, that is 

\bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\zeta (s) = \prod _{p{\text{ prime}}} \frac {1}{1-p^{-s}}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Finally, to make this proof rigorous, we need to show that when $\Re (s) > 1$, the left hand side of Equation \ref{sieved_euler_product_formula} approaches 1.
But this follows immediately from the convergence of the Dirichlet series \cite{wiki:dirichlet_series} for $\zeta(s)$. $\square$

\section{Conclusions}
As was the case with Euler's solution to the Basel problem \cite{wiki:basel}, today there are many other proofs of the Euler product formula. But Euler's proof
is (IMO of course) still the simplest and most intuitive.
