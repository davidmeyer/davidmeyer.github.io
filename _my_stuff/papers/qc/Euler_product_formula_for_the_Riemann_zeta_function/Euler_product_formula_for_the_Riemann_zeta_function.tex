\section{Introduction}
In the 1730s Leonhard Euler proved an important theorem in number theory, known today as Euler's Product Formula  \cite{euler_product_formula}, 
which exposes a deep relationship between the prime numbers and the Riemann zeta function \cite{wiki:zeta}. Euler's proof appeared in his thesis titled  
\emph{Variae observationes circa series infinitas} (Various Observations about Infinite Series), which was published by the 
St. Petersburg Academy in 1737 \cite{mactutor:history_of_calculus}. The discovery of the product formula was an important advancement in number theory 
and is perhaps the main reason why (or at least one of the main reasons why) the zeta function plays such a central role in the study of prime numbers.

\bigskip
\noindent
The Euler product formula for the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)$ is

\begin{equation}
\zeta (s)=\sum _{n=1}^{\infty }{\frac {1}{n^{s}}}=\prod _{p{\text{ prime}}}{\frac {1}{1-p^{-s}}}
\label{eqn:zeta}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Here the left hand side is the famous Riemann zeta function:

\begin{equation*}
\zeta (s) = \sum _{n=1}^{\infty } \frac {1}{n^{s}} = 1 + \frac {1}{2^{s}} + \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \cdots 
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
and the product on the right hand side extends over all prime numbers $p$:

\begin{equation*}
\prod _{p {\text{ prime}}}  \frac {1}{1-p^{-s}} = \frac {1}{1-2^{-s}} \cdot \frac {1}{1-3^{-s}} \cdot \frac {1}{1-5^{-s}}  \cdots  \frac {1}{1-p^{-s}} \cdots 
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
So how did Euler find this amazing relationship between the zeta function and the primes?

\section{Euler's Proof of the Product Formula}
The proof sketched here makes use only of simple algebra and is the method by which Euler originally discovered the product formula. Euler's proof takes advantage of a 
sieving process that is analogous to the ancient Sieve of Eratosthenes \cite{wiki:sieve_of_eratosthenes}, and proceeds as follows. 


\bigskip
\noindent
The input to the sieving process is the zeta function,  Equation \ref{eqn:zeta}:

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\zeta(s) = \sum _{n=1}^{\infty } \frac {1}{n^{s}} = 1 + \frac {1}{2^{s}} + \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \frac{1}{4^s} + \frac{1}{5^2} + \cdots    
\label{equation2}  
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
The first step of the algorithm is to multiply both sides of Equation \ref{equation2} by the second term of Equation \ref{equation2},  $\frac{1}{2^s}$,  to give
a new equation, Equation \ref{equation3}:

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\frac {1}{2^{s}} \zeta (s) = \frac {1}{2^{s}} + \frac {1}{4^{s}} + \frac {1}{6^{s}} + \frac {1}{8^{s}} + \frac {1}{10^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation3}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Next we subtract Equation \ref{equation3} from Equation \ref{equation2}, which knocks out all terms that have a factor of 2. This gives us

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\left (1- \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = 1 + \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \frac {1}{5^{s}} + \frac {1}{7^{s}} + \frac {1}{9^{s}} + \frac {1}{11^{s}} + \frac {1}{13^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation4}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Now we repeat the previous two steps with the output of the previous two steps. At this iteration that is Equation \ref{equation4}. So now  we multiply 
Equation \ref{equation4} by the the second term on the right hand side of Equation \ref{equation4}, $\frac{1}{3^s}$, which gives us

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\frac {1}{3^{s}} \left (1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = \frac {1}{3^{s}} + \frac {1}{9^{s}} + \frac {1}{15^{s}} + \frac {1}{21^{s}} + \frac {1}{27^{s}} + \frac {1}{33^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation5}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
Subtracting Equation \ref{equation5} from Equation \ref{equation4} we get

\bigskip
\begin{equation}
\left ( 1 - \frac {1}{3^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = 1 + \frac {1}{5^{s}} + \frac {1}{7^{s}} + \frac {1}{11^{s}} + \frac {1}{13^{s}} + \frac {1}{17^{s}} + \cdots 
\label{equation6}
\end{equation}

\bigskip
\noindent
which knocks out all of the terms on the right had side of Equation \ref{equation6} which have a factor of 3. The next iteration will knock out all of the terms
on the right hand side that have a factor of 5 yielding

\bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\left ( 1 - \frac {1}{5^{s}} \right )  \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{3^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = 1 + \frac {1}{7^{s}} + \frac {1}{11^{s}} + \frac {1}{13^{s}} + \frac {1}{17^{s}} + \cdots 
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\bigskip
\noindent
What we see is that this algorithm "sieves" the right hand sides of these successive equations in a process that is analogous to the 
Sieve of Eratosthenes while building up the product of $\left ( 1 - \frac{1}{p^{s}} \right)$ terms, where $p$ is prime, on the left hand side.

\bigskip
\noindent
If we repeat this process infinitely for the $\frac{1}{p^2}$ terms on the right hand side of the sieved equations we wind up with 

\begin{equation*}
\cdots \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{13^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{11^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{7^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{5^{s}} \right )
 \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{3^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \zeta (s) = 1
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
Solving for $\zeta(s)$ we get

\bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\zeta (s) = \frac {1}{\left ( 1 - \frac {1}{2^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{3^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{5^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{7^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{11^{s}} \right ) \left ( 1 - \frac {1}{13^{s}} \right )\cdots}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\bigskip
\noindent
which can be written in a perhaps more familiar (and compact) way as an infinite product over all of the primes:

\bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\zeta (s) = \prod _{p{\text{ prime}}} \frac {1}{1-p^{-s}}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
\noindent
which is Euler's product formula for the zeta function.

\bigskip
\noindent
Finally, to make this proof rigorous we need to show that when $\Re (s) > 1$ the right hand side of the sieved equations approaches 1.
Fortunately for us this follows immediately from the convergence of the Dirichlet series for $\zeta(s)$ \cite{wiki:dirichlet_series}. $\square$

\section{Conclusions}
Euler's proof of the product formula is, IMO of course, quite clever, simple and elegant (well, what would expect from Euler?). As an aside, Mathologer 
has a very nice video on this topic with some fancy animations that make it easy to see how the sieve works \cite{mathologer:eulers_product_formula}.
