\bigskip
\noindent
Why does $z = \mathrm{e}^{i \theta}$? Consider

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{lllll}
z
&=& a + ib                                          &\qquad \qquad \qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{definition of the complex plane} \\
&=&  r \cos \theta + i r \sin \theta       &\qquad \qquad \qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{definitions: $a = r \cos \theta$ and $b = r \sin \theta$} \\
&=&  r [\cos \theta +i \sin \theta]        &\qquad \qquad \qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{factor out  $r$} \\
&=& re^{i\theta}                                 &\qquad \qquad \qquad \mathrel{\#} \text{Euler's formula: }  e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i \sin \theta
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

 \bigskip
 \bigskip
\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{lllll}
e^{i\theta}
&=&                   \cos \theta + i \sin \theta              &\qquad \qquad \quad  \mathrel{\#} \text{Euler's formula}                            \\
&\Rightarrow&  e^{i\pi} = \cos \pi + i \sin \pi          &\qquad \qquad \quad  \mathrel{\#} \text{set $\theta = \pi$}                          \\
&\Rightarrow&  e^{i\pi} = -1 + i \cdot 0                  &\qquad \qquad \quad  \mathrel{\#} \text{$\cos \pi = -1$ and $\sin \pi = 0$}  \\
&\Rightarrow&  e^{i\pi} = -1 + 0                            &\qquad \qquad \quad  \mathrel{\#} i \cdot 0 = 0                                            \\
&\Rightarrow&  e^{i\pi} = -1                                  &\qquad \qquad \quad  \mathrel{\#} \text{simplify}                                          \\
&\Rightarrow&  e^{i\pi} +1 = 0                              &\qquad \qquad \quad  \mathrel{\#} \text{Euler's Identity} 
\end{array}
\end{equation*}
