\section{Introduction}
\label{section:introduction}
A Kepler triangle is named after Johannes Kepler
\cite{wikipedia:johannes_kepler}, is a right triangle whose sides
are related by a geometric progression
\cite{wiki:geometric_series,wikipedia:kepler_triangle}.

\bigskip
{\setstretch{1.15}
\noindent
If the common ratio of this geometric progression is $\sqrt{x}$,
then the sides are in the ratio $1 : \sqrt{x} : x$, so we have $1
+x = x^{2}$, or $x^{2}-x-1 = 0$. This interesting triangle involves
the golden ratio $\phi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$
\cite{wiki:golden_ratio}.

\bigskip
\noindent
Why? Well, for this triangle we can conclude that $x = \phi$,
since $x^2 -x -1$ is $\phi$'s minimal polynomial and thus has
$\phi$ as it's positive root \cite{wiki:golden_ratio}. So $x =
\phi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$, and the sides of the Kepler
triangle are in the ratio $1 : \sqrt{\phi} : \phi \approx 1 :
1.272 : 1.618$.  \par}


\bigskip
\noindent
Interestingly, a triangle with sides $k$, $k\sqrt{\phi}$, and
$k\phi$ (for $k > 0$) is also a Kepler triangle since, by the
Pythagorean theorem \cite{wiki:trig} we know that

\vspace{1.75em}

\begin{math}
\begin{array}{rcll} 
(k\phi)^{2} 
&=& (k\sqrt{\phi})^{2} + k^{2}
		&\hspace{1.25em} \mathrel{\#} \text{by the Pythagorean theorem} \\
[8pt]
&\Rightarrow& (k\phi)^{2}- (k\sqrt{\phi})^{2} - k^{2} = 0 
		&\hspace{1.25em} \mathrel{\#} \text{move everything to the left side} \\
[8pt]
&\Rightarrow& k^{2}({\phi}^{2} - \phi - 1) = 0 
		&\hspace{1.25em} \mathrel{\#} \text{factor out $k^2$} \\
[8pt]
&\Rightarrow& {\phi}^{2} - \phi - 1 = 0 
		&\hspace{1.25em} \mathrel{\#} \text{divide though by $k^2$}
\end{array}
\end{math} 

\bigskip
\noindent
And again, we know that the polynomial ${\phi}^{2} - \phi -
1$ has positive root $\phi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$. This triangle
is shown in Figure \ref{figure:kepler_triangle}.

\bigskip
